  1. Technical style points

    Broadly we follow the Economist Style Guide.

    If there are style differences for separate sections of the site, you will see a ‘Style differences’ heading under the main content; if not, this style is to be assumed across the whole of GOV.UK.

    Addressing the user

    Users benefit from simple, direct language. Address the user as ‘you’ where possible. Content on the site often makes a direct appeal to citizens and businesses to get involved or take action, eg: “You can contact HMRC by phone and email” or “Pay your car tax.”

    Style differences

    Mainstream: Don’t use ‘we’, ‘I’, ‘our’, ‘us’.

    Inside government: Use ‘we’ if the ‘we’ is obvious from the context, eg it's clearly a department or the government.

    Using plain English

    Use plain English – don't use complicated or long words when easy or short ones will do – use 'buy' instead of 'purchase', 'about' instead of 'approximately' and 'like' instead of 'such as'.

    Write content as simply as possible so that it is usable and accessible to all citizens, regardless of their language and vocabulary skills.

    Write conversationally – picture your audience and write as if you were talking to them one-to-one but with the authority of someone who can actively assist.

    Style differences

    Please note: use of plain English is mandatory for all sections of the site - all audiences should understand our content; this isn’t ‘dumbing down’, this is opening up government information to all.

    Jargon

    Avoid unnecessary jargon, legalistic prose, unexplained abbreviations or acronyms, rarely used Latin terms (inter alia, ad hoc, ibid) etc.
